# Doctoral Learning Path

This roadmap is designed for a sociology PhD student who wants to become strong in Social Network Analysis, computational sociology and graph-based research.

---

## Phase 1: Sociological foundations

Read and connect SNA to theory.

Core concepts:

- social structure
- social capital
- brokerage
- homophily
- cohesion
- fields
- diffusion
- institutions
- symbolic power
- relational sociology

Recommended authors:

- Georg Simmel
- Pierre Bourdieu
- Mark Granovetter
- Ronald Burt
- Harrison White
- Barry Wellman
- Stanley Wasserman
- Katherine Faust
- Linton Freeman
- Duncan Watts
- Mark Newman
- Matthew Jackson

Goal: understand that networks are not only data structures; they are ways of theorising the social.

---

## Phase 2: Mathematical foundations

Minimum toolkit:

- linear algebra: matrices, eigenvectors, matrix factorisation
- probability: random variables, conditional probability, Bayes
- statistics: regression, likelihood, model comparison
- optimisation: objective functions, greedy algorithms
- graph theory: paths, connectivity, components, flows
- simulation: Monte Carlo, agent-based models

Goal: read algorithmic papers without fear.

---

## Phase 3: Classical SNA

Implement and interpret:

- degree, closeness, betweenness
- eigenvector centrality, Katz, PageRank
- density, reciprocity, transitivity
- components and cliques
- structural holes
- k-core
- homophily and assortativity
- affiliation networks
- blockmodeling

Deliverable: one notebook per family, using small networks and sociological interpretation.

---

## Phase 4: Communities and meso-level structure

Study:

- Girvan-Newman
- modularity
- Louvain
- Leiden
- Infomap
- spectral clustering
- SBM
- overlapping communities

Critical question: when does an algorithmic cluster correspond to a social group?

Deliverable: compare at least five community algorithms on the same dataset.

---

## Phase 5: Diffusion and influence

Study:

- independent cascade
- linear threshold
- complex contagion
- SIR/SIS/SEIR
- influence maximisation
- peer effects
- homophily vs influence

Critical question: what evidence is needed to claim influence?

Deliverable: simulate diffusion under different network topologies.

---

## Phase 6: Statistical network modelling

Study:

- ERGM
- TERGM
- SAOM / SIENA
- latent space models
- stochastic block models
- graphons
- network autocorrelation

Deliverable: estimate an ERGM or SAOM and write a sociological interpretation of parameters.

---

## Phase 7: Temporal, multilayer and heterogeneous networks

Study:

- temporal paths
- dynamic centrality
- relational event models
- multilayer modularity
- multiplex networks
- heterogeneous information networks
- meta-paths

Deliverable: build a multiplex network from real or simulated data.

---

## Phase 8: Graph machine learning

Study:

- DeepWalk
- node2vec
- LINE
- GraphSAGE
- GCN
- GAT
- temporal GNNs
- explainability

Critical question: what is gained and lost when moving from explanation to prediction?

Deliverable: train a basic node classification model and explain its limits sociologically.

---

## Phase 9: Causal inference with networks

Study:

- interference
- spillovers
- exposure mappings
- network experiments
- endogenous tie formation
- peer effects
- causal diffusion

Deliverable: design a credible research design for distinguishing homophily from influence.

---

## Phase 10: PhD-level synthesis

Final outputs:

1. A literature review organised by algorithm family.
2. A reproducible code library.
3. A benchmark comparing algorithms.
4. A theoretical chapter on what network algorithms make visible and invisible.
5. A case study using real sociological data.
6. An ethical reflection on measurement, surveillance and algorithmic power.

---

## Suggested weekly routine

- 2 days: mathematical/algorithmic reading
- 2 days: coding and implementation
- 1 day: sociological theory and interpretation
- 1 day: writing
- 1 day: rest, review or visualisation

A strong doctoral profile requires both technical ability and conceptual clarity.

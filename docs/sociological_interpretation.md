# Sociological Interpretation Guide

This guide translates network algorithms into sociological questions.

---

## 1. Nodes are not just points

A node can represent a person, organisation, neighbourhood, school, document, hashtag, concept, event, family or institution.

Before running algorithms, define what kind of social entity the node represents.

Key question: **what is being made equivalent by being represented as a node?**

---

## 2. Edges are theoretical decisions

An edge can mean friendship, communication, trust, conflict, co-presence, collaboration, citation, kinship, exchange, similarity or shared membership.

Key question: **does the edge represent interaction, recognition, influence, similarity or institutional co-location?**

---

## 3. Centrality is not one concept

| Measure | What it may mean | What it does not necessarily mean |
|---|---|---|
| Degree | visibility, activity, popularity | power |
| Betweenness | brokerage, mediation | trustworthiness |
| Eigenvector | prestige by association | moral legitimacy |
| PageRank | recursive attention | social value |
| Closeness | reachability | actual influence |
| k-core | embeddedness | leadership |

Never write "the most important actor" without specifying the definition of importance.

---

## 4. Communities are not automatically communities

Algorithmic communities may reflect friendship groups, institutional divisions, class fractions, professional fields, platform affordances, sampling bias, geography, homophily, administrative categories or data collection artefacts.

Interpretation requires external validation.

Validation strategies:

- qualitative interviews
- ethnography
- attribute comparison
- temporal stability
- comparison across algorithms
- expert review
- robustness checks

---

## 5. Brokerage and inequality

Brokerage can be interpreted through:

- Granovetter: weak ties as bridges to new information
- Burt: structural holes and competitive advantage
- Bourdieu: capital, field position and symbolic power
- Simmel: the stranger, triads and mediation

Possible research questions:

- Who connects institutional worlds?
- Who translates between social groups?
- Who benefits from being between disconnected actors?
- Who performs invisible coordination work?

---

## 6. Homophily and social reproduction

Homophily asks whether similar actors are more likely to be connected.

Attributes may include gender, class, age, ethnicity, education, profession, ideology, diagnosis, geography or institution.

Sociological caution: homophily can result from preference, opportunity, segregation, institutional design or algorithmic recommendation. It should not be reduced to individual choice.

---

## 7. Diffusion is not always influence

A behaviour can spread because of direct influence, shared environment, homophily, institutional pressure, media exposure, algorithmic amplification, common shocks or selection effects.

A diffusion curve alone does not prove influence.

---

## 8. Prediction is not explanation

Graph machine learning can predict links, labels, communities, anomalies and recommendations. But prediction does not automatically explain why a tie forms, why inequality persists, why a group boundary matters or what a relation means to participants.

For doctoral sociology, prediction should be connected to theory, mechanism and interpretation.

---

## 9. Missing data is social

Missing ties may reflect privacy, stigma, non-response, platform API limits, institutional invisibility, informal relations, hidden labour, fear, exclusion or measurement design.

Absence in the graph is not the same as absence in social life.

---

## 10. Ethical questions

Before publishing network analysis, ask:

- Can individuals or vulnerable groups be re-identified?
- Does the graph expose sensitive relations?
- Could centrality rankings harm participants?
- Are communities being labelled responsibly?
- Are algorithmic outputs being overinterpreted?
- Does the research reproduce surveillance logics?

A sociological network analysis should be technically rigorous and ethically reflexive.
